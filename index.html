In 2007, Alameda County, California built a new Juvenile Justice Center, a $176 million complex of courtrooms, law offices, and a huge, 360-bed juvenile hall. Christian Munoz is taking me inside Juvenile Hall where he works as the Superintendent. He says he’s having trouble keeping the facility staffed.

“We survive on overtime, it’s that bad,” he said.

Ironically, the juvenile headcount is at an all-time low in almost five years. Still, overtime costs are soaring here -- they’re up about $6 million annually, which is more than double what it was five years ago.

Just minutes into showing me around, an announcement squawked into Munoz’s walkie talkie:

“If you’re interested or available to work, please give me a call in the junior-seniors office.”

The evening shift was starting in less than an hour and Munoz was short six people. He said this happens all the time. There are lots of reasons for the staffing shortage: a wave of retirements; a slew of guards promoted into the adult system; guards filling in for workers comp. Across the state, hiring into the juvenile system is a challenge. Background checks often eliminate candidates because of past criminal activity or for having bad credit.

Unit 1 is two stories high with 15 cells on each level. There were three guards on duty: two working the upper and lower decks, shuffling kids back and forth from their cells to the showers. They were also doing room checks, looking for contraband the kids have hidden away.

A teen named Rudy started yelling. He had just returned to his cell to discover the cookies and snacks he had stashed away were confiscated. As punishment for having food in his cell, he got docked 15 minutes of rec time. He refused to go back in his room.

Bonnie Lacy was one of the guards working in Unit 1. She walked towards Rudy, making eye contact. “Fifteen minutes for me,” she told Rudy. With that, Rudy turned around, stepped into his cell, and closed the door.

The superintendent and several guards told me they prefer to talk through conflicts like this with kids. But incidents can escalate quickly. In Alameda County, with guards working an average of 30 hours of overtime per week, there has been an increase in the use of force on juvenile inmates. Guards perform takedowns or handcuff inmates when there is a confrontation. The department calls these acts “use of physical and mechanical restraints,” and that number is up nearly 300 percent.

"There are only about 150 kids at Alameda County Juvenile Hall at any given time, and it costs 44 million dollars a year to detain them."

During the day, the state mandates a minimum of three guards in a 30-cell unit like this one. But, Cologne said this facility sometimes falls short. When they’re short on guards, supervisors sometimes run what they call “split recs” which divides recreation, exercise, and dinner time in half. This means that if there is one hour of rec time, 15 kids come out for recreation for 30 minutes, while the other 15 remain in their cells, before switching.

Being inside a 6x10 foot cell can take a toll on an inmate’s mental health. Eighteen-year-old Jason, spent more than a month in juvenile hall last year.

“It would kind of like make you go insane. You know it would make you really feel like you were just stuck in a small box and you just will never get out. That like kinda caused me to not like really tight rooms,” he said.
